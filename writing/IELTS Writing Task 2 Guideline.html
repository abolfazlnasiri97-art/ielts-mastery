💡 Strategy

⭐ The 5 Golden Rules

  • Answer every part of the question — if it asks for a discussion AND your opinion, do both. Partial answers cap your Task Response score.
  • Take a clear position — and keep it consistent from introduction to conclusion. Don't sit on the fence unless the question explicitly allows balance.
  • One main idea per paragraph — support it with an explanation and a specific example. Don't cram multiple ideas into one paragraph.
  • Use linking words naturally — overusing "Moreover" and "Furthermore" in every sentence looks mechanical, not sophisticated.
  • Plan before you write — 4-5 minutes of planning prevents you from contradicting yourself or running out of ideas halfway through.
Foundation

The 4-Paragraph Essay Structure

A consistent, logical structure is the backbone of every high-band Task 2 response. The exact content of body paragraphs shifts by essay type, but this skeleton works for all of them.

🏗️ Paragraph-by-Paragraph Breakdown

Paragraph 1 — Introduction

Paraphrase + Thesis statement

Sentence 1: paraphrase the question topic in your own words. Sentence 2: state your thesis — your overall position or a clear outline of what the essay will cover. The examiner should know your stance (or that you'll discuss both sides) by the end of paragraph 1.

🔁 "Some people believe…" → "It is often argued that…" / "A growing number of people contend that…"
Paragraph 2 — Body 1 ⭐ FIRST MAIN IDEA

One idea, explained, then exemplified

Topic sentence stating the main idea. Then explain WHY or HOW it's true (the "extension"). Then give a specific, concrete example — a real-world scenario, not another vague restatement. This explain-then-exemplify pattern is what separates Band 5 from Band 7.

⚠️ A paragraph with no example is the single biggest cause of low Task Response scores.
Paragraph 3 — Body 2

Second main idea (or the counter-argument)

Same explain-then-exemplify pattern as Body 1. If the question requires discussing both sides, this is usually where you present the opposing view before returning to your own opinion. Use linking language to connect back to paragraph 2.

📌 Keep ideas distinct — don't repeat the point you already made in Body 1 with different words.
Paragraph 4 — Conclusion

Summarise and restate your position

One to two sentences. Summarise the main points covered (in different words from your introduction) and restate your overall opinion clearly. Do NOT introduce any new ideas or examples here — that confuses the examiner about your argument's structure.

🔗 Start with: "In conclusion…", "To conclude…", "Overall, it is clear that…"

✍️ Sample Structured Response Band 7

Question: "Some people think the government should ban dangerous sports, while others believe people should have the freedom to do any sport they want. Discuss both views and give your own opinion." Colours indicate: thesis/position example linking

Introduction: Whether dangerous sports such as bungee jumping or boxing should be prohibited by law is a contentious issue. While there are valid arguments for restricting such activities, this essay will argue that individuals should retain the freedom to choose, provided they are fully informed of the risks.

Body 1 (opposing view): On the one hand, proponents of a ban point to the significant public costs associated with extreme sports injuries. For example, emergency services in mountainous regions of Switzerland report that a disproportionate share of their annual budget is spent rescuing injured climbers and skiers, a cost ultimately borne by taxpayers who never chose to take such risks.

Body 2 (own view): On the other hand, restricting personal freedom in this way sets a troubling precedent. In countries like New Zealand, where bungee jumping and skydiving are heavily regulated rather than banned outright, strict safety certification has reduced accident rates while still allowing enthusiasts to participate, suggesting that regulation is a more balanced solution than prohibition.

Conclusion: In conclusion, although dangerous sports carry real risks and costs, an outright ban is a disproportionate response. Robust regulation, rather than prohibition, better balances public safety with individual freedom.

✅ Ideal Word Count

Minimum: 250 words (penalties if below)
Ideal: 270–320 words
Maximum: No hard limit, but 350+ often means losing focus or running out of time to proofread

Quality over quantity. A focused 280-word essay with two well-developed ideas beats a rambling 400-word essay with five underdeveloped ones.

🚫 What NOT to Include

  • Memorised "template" sentences that don't fit the specific question
  • A list of unrelated ideas with no development
  • New arguments or examples in the conclusion
  • An unclear or shifting position on the topic
  • Generic examples that could apply to any essay ("for example, in today's society…")

🧩 Planning Your Essay (4–5 minutes)

  • Step 1 — Identify the question type: Opinion, Discussion, Problem/Solution, Advantages/Disadvantages, or Direct Question. This determines your structure.
  • Step 2 — Decide your position: For opinion essays, choose a side now — don't decide as you write.
  • Step 3 — Brainstorm 2 ideas per body paragraph slot: one strong idea is better than three weak ones.
  • Step 4 — Think of one specific example per idea: a real example, a hypothetical scenario, or a piece of common knowledge — but make it concrete.
  • Step 5 — Sketch your thesis sentence: write it mentally before you touch the introduction, so the whole essay stays consistent.
Essay Types

All 5 Task 2 Essay Types

Each question type requires a different essay structure and approach. Learn to identify the type from the wording, then apply the specific strategies, language, and pitfalls for each.

💭

Opinion Essays (Agree/Disagree)

The question asks "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" or presents one view and asks for your opinion. You must take a clear, consistent stance — fully agree, fully disagree, or a partial position — and defend it throughout.

🔍 How to Recognise This Type

Look for phrases like: "To what extent do you agree or disagree?", "Do you agree or disagree with this statement?", or "What is your opinion?" following a single claim or viewpoint.

🎯 Strategies

  • Decide your position before writing — full agreement, full disagreement, or partial agreement
  • State your position clearly in the introduction and never contradict it later
  • If taking a partial position, dedicate one body paragraph to each side, but make your final lean clear
  • Every body paragraph should support your stated position with reasoning and an example
  • Avoid simply re-explaining the statement — add your own reasoning

⚠️ Pitfalls

  • Agreeing in the introduction but disagreeing (or being vague) in the body
  • Writing a "balanced" essay with no real opinion when the question demands one
  • Discussing the opposite view without explaining why you still disagree with it
  • Treating it like a discussion essay structure when full agreement was more natural

🏗️ Recommended Structure

Intro: Paraphrase + clear thesis stating your position.
Body 1: First reason supporting your view + example.
Body 2: Second reason supporting your view + example (or, for partial agreement, the aspect you concede).
Conclusion: Restate your position and summarise your reasons.

✍️ Sample Band 7 Thesis & Body Opener

Question: "Some people believe that unpaid community service should be a compulsory part of high school programmes. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"

I completely agree that mandatory community service should form part of secondary education, as it cultivates civic responsibility and provides practical benefits that classroom learning alone cannot offer.

To begin with, compulsory volunteering exposes teenagers to social realities beyond their immediate environment. For instance, students who volunteer at homeless shelters or food banks often develop a far deeper understanding of poverty than they would from a textbook chapter alone.

📝 Full Band 7 Sample Answer

Prompt: "Some people think that universities should provide graduates with the knowledge and skills needed in the workplace. Others think that the true function of a university should be to give access to knowledge for its own sake, regardless of whether the course is useful to an employer. What, in your opinion, should be the main function of a university?"

Introduction: The purpose of higher education has long been a subject of debate. While some argue that universities should primarily prepare students for the labour market, I firmly believe that their core function remains the pursuit of knowledge for its own sake, with practical skills as a secondary outcome.

Body 1 (opposing view): Admittedly, there is a strong economic argument for universities aligning their curricula with the needs of employers. Graduates who enter the workforce equipped with relevant skills tend to secure employment more quickly and earn higher salaries, which in turn boosts tax revenues and reduces unemployment. From this standpoint, treating universities as engines of human capital appears both efficient and socially responsible.

Body 2 (own view): However, reducing higher education to vocational training overlooks its deeper purpose. Universities have historically served as spaces in which knowledge is pursued, contested, and advanced, often in ways whose practical value only becomes clear decades later. Many of the technologies that drive today's economy — from quantum computing to mRNA vaccines — emerged from theoretical research that no employer had specifically requested. Furthermore, an education grounded in critical enquiry equips graduates to adapt to a rapidly changing labour market in which today's specialist skills may be obsolete within a decade. By contrast, narrowly vocational courses risk producing graduates who struggle when their industry shifts.

Conclusion: In my view, the wisest approach is one in which universities preserve their commitment to knowledge while ensuring students develop transferable skills such as analysis, communication, and independent research. Employment outcomes will follow naturally from a rigorous education, whereas the reverse — reducing universities to training centres — would impoverish both scholarship and, ultimately, the economy that depends on it.

Discussion Essays (Discuss Both Views)

The question presents two opposing views and asks you to "discuss both views and give your own opinion." Unlike opinion essays, you must give genuine, balanced coverage to BOTH sides before stating your own view.

🔍 How to Recognise This Type

Look for: "Discuss both these views and give your own opinion." The question will present two views, often as "Some people think X. Others believe Y."

🎯 Strategies

  • Dedicate one full body paragraph to each view — give both genuine, fair treatment
  • Don't dismiss either view too quickly; explain why reasonable people hold it
  • State your own opinion clearly — in the introduction, conclusion, or woven through the body
  • Use clear signalling language: "One view is that…" / "Others, however, argue that…"
  • Your opinion can favour one side, partially agree with both, or weigh them — but it must be clear

⚠️ Pitfalls

  • Forgetting to give your own opinion (a required, separate task element)
  • Giving one view a full paragraph and dismissing the other in one sentence
  • Writing two viewpoints with no examples for either
  • Confusing this with an opinion essay and only arguing one side throughout

🏗️ Recommended Structure

Intro: Paraphrase + state that you will discuss both views, optionally hint at your own.
Body 1: First view, explained fairly + example.
Body 2: Second view, explained fairly + example, then your own opinion if not already stated.
Conclusion: Summarise both views and restate your own opinion clearly.

✍️ Sample Band 7 Thesis & Both Views

Question: "Some people think that the best way to reduce crime is to give longer prison sentences. Others, however, believe there are better alternative ways to do this. Discuss both views and give your own opinion."

While longer prison terms are often seen as an effective deterrent, this essay will argue that rehabilitation-focused alternatives are ultimately more successful at reducing reoffending.

On the one hand, advocates of longer sentencing argue that removing offenders from society for extended periods directly prevents further crime. On the other hand, critics point out that prison often reinforces criminal behaviour rather than correcting it. Norway's prison system, for example, focuses on education and job training, and reports reoffending rates roughly a third of those in countries relying on long sentences alone.

📝 Full Band 7 Sample Answer

Prompt: "Some people think that the internet brings people closer together, while others believe it isolates us from one another. Discuss both views and give your own opinion."

Introduction: The rise of the internet has fundamentally reshaped how people interact, prompting disagreement over whether it ultimately brings individuals closer together or pushes them apart. In my view, while the internet can encourage isolation when used without restraint, its overall effect has been to connect people in ways that were previously impossible.

Body 1 (first view): On the one hand, critics argue that the internet fosters social isolation. Many users, particularly young people, spend hours each day scrolling through social media, often at the expense of face-to-face interaction. Studies have linked heavy social media use with higher rates of anxiety and loneliness, suggesting that digital connections can be a poor substitute for genuine human contact. Furthermore, online algorithms tend to expose users to content that reinforces their existing views, creating echo chambers that can polarise communities and erode shared understanding.

Body 2 (second view + own opinion): On the other hand, the internet has clearly expanded the boundaries of human connection. Families separated by continents can now video-call one another free of charge, something that was prohibitively expensive only a generation ago. Online communities allow individuals with niche interests or rare medical conditions to find support that would have been unavailable locally. During the pandemic, the internet enabled remote work, online education, and virtual socialising that prevented total isolation for millions of people. From this perspective, the technology itself is neither connecting nor isolating; its effects depend largely on how it is used.

Conclusion: In conclusion, the internet's capacity to connect outweighs its risks, provided that users cultivate deliberate habits and that platforms are regulated to limit manipulation. With mindful use, the same tool that can isolate individuals can also sustain relationships across oceans.
🧩

Problem/Solution Essays

The question describes a problem and asks you to identify causes and/or propose solutions. Sometimes phrased as "problems and solutions," sometimes as "causes and effects." Read the exact wording carefully — it determines your two body paragraphs.

🔍 How to Recognise This Type

Look for: "What are the causes of this problem and what solutions can you suggest?", "What problems does this cause and how can they be solved?", or "What are the causes and effects?" The exact pairing (causes+solutions vs. problems+solutions vs. causes+effects) tells you what each body paragraph must cover.

🎯 Strategies

  • Match your paragraphs exactly to what's asked — don't give two causes when one cause + one solution was requested
  • Keep causes and solutions logically connected — a solution should plausibly address the cause you raised
  • Be specific: "lack of education" is vague; "limited access to driver safety courses in rural areas" is specific
  • You don't need to state a personal opinion unless the question explicitly asks for one
  • Use cause/effect and recommendation language naturally throughout

⚠️ Pitfalls

  • Listing many causes or solutions superficially instead of developing 1-2 well
  • Proposing a solution that doesn't logically address the cause you described
  • Adding an unrequested opinion paragraph that isn't part of the task
  • Being too vague: "people should be educated" without saying how or by whom

🏗️ Recommended Structure

Intro: Paraphrase the problem + outline that you'll cover causes/effects and solutions.
Body 1: Cause(s) or problem(s), explained + example.
Body 2: Solution(s) or effect(s), explained + example.
Conclusion: Summarise the cause-solution (or problem-effect) link and give a brief final thought.

✍️ Sample Band 7 Cause & Solution Paragraphs

Question: "In many cities, traffic congestion is a growing problem. What are the causes of this problem, and what measures could be taken to solve it?"

One of the primary causes of traffic congestion is the heavy reliance on private vehicles for daily commuting. In cities like Bangkok, where public transport coverage remains limited in suburban areas, the vast majority of commuters have little choice but to drive, overwhelming road capacity during peak hours.

A practical solution to this issue would be substantial investment in affordable, reliable public transit. Seoul's expansion of its metro network over the past two decades, for example, coincided with a measurable shift away from private car use, demonstrating that accessible alternatives can directly reduce congestion.

📝 Full Band 7 Sample Answer

Prompt: "In many countries, the amount of crime is increasing. What do you think are the main causes of crime? How can we deal with those causes?"

Introduction: Rising crime rates have become a pressing concern in many countries, prompting debate about its underlying causes and the most effective ways to address it. In my view, the principal drivers of crime are economic inequality and inadequate access to education, and these problems require coordinated social investment rather than purely punitive responses.

Body 1 (causes): The first major cause of crime is economic deprivation. When individuals face long-term unemployment, low wages, or homelessness, the gap between legitimate opportunity and illicit gain narrows considerably. In neighbourhoods where stable employment is scarce and public services are underfunded, crime often becomes a rational, if illegal, means of survival. A further contributing factor is the failure of education systems to engage the most disadvantaged students. Young people who leave school without qualifications are statistically far more likely to become involved in criminal activity, as they lack both the skills for legitimate work and the social bonds that schooling provides.

Body 2 (solutions): Addressing these root causes requires sustained investment in both economic and educational opportunity. Governments should prioritise vocational training and apprenticeship programmes targeted at young people in deprived areas, giving them a realistic pathway into stable employment. At the same time, schools in disadvantaged communities need additional funding to provide mentoring, mental health support, and extracurricular activities that keep students engaged. Several Latin American cities, including Bogotá, have shown that combining youth programmes with community policing can reduce violent crime significantly within a few years.

Conclusion: In conclusion, crime is rarely the result of individual moral failure alone; it is most often a symptom of deeper social inequalities. Tackling it effectively means investing in people rather than merely expanding prisons.

Advantages/Disadvantages Essays

The question asks you to weigh the benefits and drawbacks of a situation or trend. Two sub-types exist: neutral ("discuss the advantages and disadvantages") and opinion-based ("do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?").

🔍 How to Recognise This Type

Look for: "What are the advantages and disadvantages of this?" (neutral — no personal opinion needed) vs. "Do you think the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" (opinion required — you must state which side wins).

🎯 Strategies

  • First, check whether the question is neutral or asks you to weigh the two — this changes whether you need a personal opinion
  • If neutral: one paragraph per side, both presented fairly, with no need to favour one
  • If "outweigh": still cover both sides, but your thesis and conclusion must clearly state which is greater
  • Use comparative and weighing language especially in the "outweigh" version
  • Choose advantages/disadvantages relevant to the specific topic, not generic ones

⚠️ Pitfalls

  • Giving a personal opinion when the question only asked for a neutral discussion
  • Failing to state which side outweighs the other when explicitly asked
  • Listing disconnected advantages/disadvantages without development or examples
  • Spending unequal space on each side without a clear reason

🏗️ Recommended Structure

Intro: Paraphrase + outline (neutral) or paraphrase + thesis stating which side outweighs (opinion version).
Body 1: Advantage(s), explained + example.
Body 2: Disadvantage(s), explained + example (with a weighing comment if "outweigh" was asked).
Conclusion: Summarise both sides; for "outweigh" questions, restate your final judgement.

✍️ Sample Band 7 Weighing Language (Outweigh Version)

Question: "Many people now work from home rather than in an office. Do the advantages of this trend outweigh the disadvantages?"

Although remote work offers genuine drawbacks in terms of collaboration, this essay will argue that its benefits for both employees and the environment ultimately outweigh these concerns.

On balance, the flexibility afforded by remote work brings advantages that are difficult to replicate in a traditional office. A 2022 survey by a major UK recruitment firm found that employees working from home reported saving an average of nine hours per week previously lost to commuting — time redirected toward family, exercise, or further study. While it is true that spontaneous collaboration suffers without face-to-face contact, this is a manageable cost rather than a decisive one.

📝 Full Band 7 Sample Answer

Prompt: "In many countries, traditional food is being replaced by international fast food. Do the advantages of this trend outweigh the disadvantages?"

Introduction: The spread of international fast food has transformed eating habits in many countries, particularly in urban areas. While some welcome the convenience and affordability that global chains offer, others worry about the cultural and health consequences. In my view, the disadvantages of this trend clearly outweigh the advantages.

Body 1 (advantages): Admittedly, there are some genuine benefits to the rise of international fast food. The most obvious is convenience: for working families with limited time, the ability to purchase a hot meal quickly and cheaply can be a practical lifeline. In addition, global chains often introduce hygiene and quality standards that influence local competitors, raising expectations across the sector. They also create employment, both directly in restaurants and indirectly in supply chains.

Body 2 (disadvantages + weighing): However, these benefits are overshadowed by serious drawbacks. From a health perspective, the high levels of salt, sugar, and fat in most fast food are closely linked to rising rates of obesity, diabetes, and heart disease, placing enormous burdens on public healthcare systems. From a cultural standpoint, the dominance of standardised international menus threatens traditional cuisines that have developed over centuries and that often reflect local ingredients, climate, and history. When global brands displace local eateries, communities lose more than recipes — they lose a piece of their identity. Furthermore, the economic benefits are often overstated: profits typically flow out of the country to multinational parent companies, while local food producers are squeezed out of the market.

Conclusion: In conclusion, although international fast food offers convenience and jobs, its long-term costs to public health, cultural diversity, and local economies are too significant to ignore. The trend should therefore be resisted through regulation, education, and the active promotion of traditional food cultures.
❓

Direct Question Essays (Two-Part / Mixed)

The question asks two distinct, specific questions about a topic, without a clear "agree/disagree" or "discuss both views" frame. Sometimes called "two-part questions." Each question becomes its own body paragraph.

🔍 How to Recognise This Type

Look for two separate question marks covering different angles, e.g.: "Why do you think this is happening? What can be done to address it?" or "Is this a positive or negative development? Why do some people prefer it?" — note this differs from problem/solution because the two questions may not be a cause-and-effect pair at all.

🎯 Strategies

  • Identify exactly what each of the two questions is asking — they are often NOT the same as cause/solution
  • Answer Question 1 fully in body paragraph 1, Question 2 fully in body paragraph 2
  • Don't blend the two questions into one vague paragraph — examiners check both are answered distinctly
  • Your introduction should briefly signal that you'll address both questions
  • A personal opinion is only needed if one of the two questions explicitly asks for it

⚠️ Pitfalls

  • Answering only one of the two questions in depth and rushing the other
  • Treating it like a standard opinion essay when no opinion was actually requested
  • Merging both questions into a single confused paragraph
  • Misreading subtly different question pairs (e.g., "why" vs. "should")

🏗️ Recommended Structure

Intro: Paraphrase the topic + briefly preview that both questions will be addressed.
Body 1: Direct, full answer to Question 1 + example.
Body 2: Direct, full answer to Question 2 + example.
Conclusion: Briefly summarise both answers together.

✍️ Sample Band 7 Two-Question Handling

Question: "In many countries, fewer young people are choosing to read for pleasure. Why do you think this is happening? What can be done to encourage reading among young people?"

This decline can largely be attributed to the rise of short-form digital entertainment, but a combination of school and family initiatives could help reverse the trend.

The first and most significant reason for this shift is competition from smartphones and social media. Where a teenager in the 1990s might have read a novel during a commute, today the same time is typically filled with short videos that demand far less sustained attention.

To address this trend, schools could integrate more student-chosen reading material into the curriculum rather than relying solely on prescribed texts. Programmes in Finland that allow students to select their own reading material have reportedly improved both reading frequency and comprehension scores among teenagers.

📝 Full Band 7 Sample Answer

Prompt: "In some parts of the world, traditional festivals are losing their significance. Why is this happening? What can be done to maintain their importance?"

Introduction: Across many parts of the world, traditional festivals that once played a central role in community life are steadily losing their cultural significance. This essay will argue that the main cause of this decline is the rapid pace of urbanisation and globalisation, and that meaningful revival will require both school-based education and active government support.

Body 1 (Question 1 — why): The primary reason for the declining importance of traditional festivals is the social disruption caused by urbanisation and globalisation. As younger generations move from close-knit rural communities to cities, they leave behind the extended family networks in which festivals were traditionally celebrated. Long working hours and the pressure of modern urban life leave little time for the elaborate preparation that such occasions require. At the same time, globalised media promotes a homogenised popular culture that competes directly with local traditions for the attention of young people. As a result, many festivals are reduced to brief, commercialised events stripped of their original meaning.

Body 2 (Question 2 — what can be done): Reversing this trend requires a combination of educational and policy measures. Schools could play a central role by integrating the history and meaning of local festivals into the curriculum, so that children grow up understanding their cultural significance rather than simply participating in them out of habit. Governments, for their part, could provide grants for community-led festival activities, subsidise traditional arts and crafts associated with these events, and designate certain festivals as protected cultural heritage. South Korea's successful designation of its harvest festival, Chuseok, as a major public holiday with state-supported programming offers a useful model.

Conclusion: In conclusion, the fading of traditional festivals is a predictable consequence of modernisation, but it is not inevitable. With conscious effort from schools, communities, and governments, these celebrations can regain their cultural importance.

⚠️ Using Linking Words Naturally

  • Don't start every single sentence with a linking word — it reads as mechanical, not sophisticated
  • Don't use "Moreover" and "Furthermore" interchangeably in the same paragraph — vary your addition language
  • Avoid heavy connectors like "Notwithstanding this" unless you're confident in the surrounding grammar
  • A well-placed linking word inside a sentence (not just at the start) often sounds more natural: "The policy, however, failed to account for…"

Master these patterns to improve your Grammatical Range & Accuracy score and express nuanced argument and opinion.

🔗 Complex Sentence Structures

Concession + Contrast (While / Although / Whereas)

While automation has improved efficiency, it has also displaced millions of workers worldwide.
Although the policy aims to reduce inequality, critics argue that it disproportionately benefits the wealthy.
✦ Use these to weigh two sides of an argument in one sentence — a hallmark of Band 7+ writing.

Conditional Structures (for solutions and consequences)

If stricter emissions regulations were introduced, air quality in major cities would improve significantly.
Were governments to invest more heavily in renewable energy, dependence on fossil fuels would decline sharply.
✦ Second conditionals are ideal for hypothetical solutions in problem/solution essays.

Relative Clauses (for adding detail concisely)

Countries that invest heavily in early childhood education tend to see long-term economic benefits.
Remote work, which has become increasingly common since the pandemic, raises new questions about work-life balance.
✦ Relative clauses let you pack extra information into a single sentence without starting a new one.

Nominalisation (turning verbs/adjectives into nouns)

The implementation of stricter regulations could reduce pollution levels. (instead of "If we implement stricter regulations…")
The reduction in working hours has led to improved employee wellbeing.
✦ Nominalisation creates a more formal, academic tone appropriate for Task 2 essays.

Hedging Language (for cautious, academic claims)

This trend is likely to continue unless governments intervene.
It could be argued that social media has done more harm than good to teenage mental health.
✦ Avoid absolute claims like "this is always true" — hedging language sounds more academically credible.

Strategy

Time Management

The single most underestimated skill in IELTS Writing Task 2 is time management. Most candidates who score below their potential do so not because they lack knowledge, but because they run out of time, leave essays unfinished, or skip the proofreading stage. ⏱ The 40-Minute Breakdown

💡 The Golden Rule: Never skip the planning phase to "save time". Candidates who plan write faster, not slower, because they never sit frozen mid-essay wondering what to say next. Equally, never skip the checking phase — most Band 6 to Band 7 jumps happen in those final 5 minutes, when candidates catch the small errors that drag down their Grammatical Range and Accuracy score.

📝 Phase 1: Planning (Minutes 0–5)

Use these 5 minutes to decode the question and plan your response. Do not start writing until you have completed all four sub-steps below.

1

Decode the question (30 seconds)

Identify the essay type (Opinion, Discussion, Problem/Solution, Adv/Disadv, Direct Question) and underline every part you must answer. Check whether a personal opinion is required. Misreading the question is the single most common reason for low Task Response scores.

2

Generate 6 raw ideas (1 minute)

Write down six possible points about the topic — three on each side for discussion/opinion essays, or three causes + three solutions for problem/solution. Quantity over quality at this stage.

3

Push each idea one layer deeper (2 minutes)

For each raw idea, ask "Why?" or "How?" once. This is the step that separates Band 6 from Band 7. Most Band 6 paragraphs say what they think; Band 7+ paragraphs explain why they think it.

4

Sketch your paragraph plan (1.5 minutes)

Write a one-line outline for each of your four paragraphs: Intro (paraphrase + thesis), Body 1 (point 1 + example), Body 2 (point 2 + example), Conclusion (restate position). Select only your two strongest points; drop the rest.

🔍 Phase 3: Checking (Minutes 35–40)

The final 5 minutes are where Band 6 essays become Band 7 essays. Most candidates finish writing, breathe a sigh of relief, and submit — leaving careless errors that drag down their Grammatical Range and Accuracy score. Use this checklist:

  • Subject-verb agreement — check that singular subjects have singular verbs and vice versa. This is the most common error in IELTS essays.
  • Articles (a, an, the) — check each noun for the correct article. Article errors are particularly common for speakers of languages that do not use articles.
  • Tense consistency — make sure you have not slipped between present, past, and future tenses within the same paragraph.
  • Spelling — look for common IELTS spelling errors: "developement" → "development", "enviroment" → "environment", "goverment" → "government".
  • Word repetition — if you have used the same word three times, find a synonym. Variety in vocabulary directly affects your Lexical Resource score.
  • Linking word placement — check that "However", "Furthermore" and similar connectors are followed by commas, and that you have not used the same connector twice in a row.
  • Question coverage — re-read the question and confirm you have answered every part. For discussion essays, have you given your own opinion as well as both views?
  • Word count — confirm you have written at least 250 words. Aim for 280–320 to develop ideas properly without padding.

📊 Pacing Strategies

Even with a plan, pacing is hard. Use these strategies to stay on track:

If you fall behind

  • Shorten your introduction and conclusion — these can be 2 sentences each if necessary.
  • Drop a secondary example rather than a main point.
  • Never skip the conclusion — an essay without one caps your Coherence score at Band 6.
  • Reduce checking time to 2 minutes rather than skipping it entirely.

If you finish early

  • Do not add a new paragraph — instead, develop existing points more fully.
  • Upgrade vocabulary: replace simple words with more precise alternatives.
  • Add a complex sentence to a paragraph that has only simple ones.
  • Check each sentence for one final error pass.

🎯 Practice Schedule

Time management is a skill that must be practised. Use this 4-week schedule to build reliable habits before exam day:

Week 1: Planning only

Take 10 prompts and practise only the 5-minute planning phase. Do not write the essays. Goal: train yourself to produce a usable outline in 5 minutes flat.

Week 2: Untimed essays

Write 3-4 full essays with no time limit. Focus on structure, development, and quality. Goal: build the writing habit and develop your repertoire of examples.

Week 3: 50-minute essays

Write 3-4 essays in 50 minutes (10 extra). Goal: practise the full sequence without time pressure, internalising the rhythm of plan-write-check.

Week 4: Full 40-minute simulation

Write 4-5 essays in exactly 40 minutes, ideally at the same time of day as your exam. Use the timer in the Practice Prompts page. Goal: replicate exam conditions as closely as possible.

💡 Exam-day tip: Use the timer in the Practice Prompts page during your week 4 simulations. Hearing the alarm at the 5-minute and 1-minute marks trains you to react calmly when time is short, rather than panicking and producing rushed, error-strewn work.

Common Mistakes & How to Fix Them

These are the most frequent errors that prevent students from reaching Band 7. Learn to spot and avoid each one.

❌ Mistake 1: Not Answering the Full Question

Many questions have two or more parts (e.g., "discuss both views AND give your opinion"). Missing any part caps your Task Response score, no matter how well-written the rest is.

✗ Wrong
Discusses both views thoroughly but never states a personal opinion, despite the question asking for one.
✓ Correct
Discusses both views AND clearly states: "In my view, while both arguments have merit, X is ultimately more persuasive because…"

❌ Mistake 2: Vague, Generic Examples

"For example, in today's society, this is very common" gives no real support. Examples should be specific and concrete, even if invented or general knowledge.

✗ Wrong
"For example, many people experience this nowadays in their daily lives."
✓ Correct
"For example, office workers who switched to remote work during the pandemic often reported working longer hours due to blurred boundaries between work and home life."

❌ Mistake 3: Shifting or Unclear Position

If you say you "agree" in the introduction but your body paragraphs mostly argue the opposite, the examiner cannot follow your argument — this damages both Task Response and Coherence.

✗ Wrong
Intro: "I completely agree." Body 1: argues against the statement. Body 2: argues for it. Conclusion: unclear which side won.
✓ Correct
Intro states a position; every body paragraph supports that position (or clearly signals it's the counter-view before returning to it); conclusion restates the same position.

❌ Mistake 4: Overusing Memorised Phrases

Examiners are trained to spot generic, memorised "template" sentences that don't actually respond to the specific question. They can lower your score for not engaging with the task.

✗ Wrong
"Nowadays, with the rapid development of society, this issue has become a hot topic which is widely discussed by people from all walks of life."
✓ Correct
"As cities expand and commuting times grow, the question of whether remote work should become the norm has gained particular relevance."

❌ Mistake 5: New Ideas in the Conclusion

The conclusion should summarise what you've already argued — introducing a brand-new point here suggests poor planning and disrupts the essay's logical structure.

✗ Wrong
"In conclusion, I agree with the statement. Furthermore, another important factor is government funding, which I haven't mentioned yet but is also very significant."
✓ Correct
"In conclusion, while economic concerns are valid, the long-term environmental benefits make stricter regulation the more sensible approach."

❌ Mistake 6: Mechanical Overuse of Linking Words

Starting every sentence with "Moreover," "Furthermore," or "In addition" makes writing feel like a checklist rather than a natural argument. Cohesion comes from logical flow, not just connector words.

✗ Wrong
"Moreover, pollution is increasing. Furthermore, this is bad for health. In addition, governments should act."
✓ Correct
"Rising pollution levels are already taking a toll on public health, which is precisely why governments must act now."

💡 Final Tips for Band 7+

  • Identify the essay type in the first 30 seconds — it determines your entire structure.
  • State your position in the introduction and never lose it — re-read your thesis before each body paragraph.
  • Every main idea needs a specific example — invented but plausible examples are perfectly acceptable.
  • Vary your sentence openers — not every sentence needs to start with the subject or a linking word.
  • Use a mix of simple and complex sentences — an essay of only complex sentences can become hard to follow.
  • Save 3-4 minutes to check that you've answered every part of the question — this is the single highest-impact final check.
  • Avoid absolute words like "always" and "never" unless you're certain — hedging language sounds more credible.

📋 The Prompt

Some people think that all university students should study whatever they like. Others believe that they should only be allowed to study subjects that will be useful in the future, such as those related to science and technology. Discuss both these views and give your own opinion.

Band 5.0 Modest user · ~240 words

Overall assessment

The response addresses the task only partially. The position is unclear and the development of ideas is limited. Vocabulary is basic and repetitive, and grammatical structures are simple with frequent errors. Cohesion is weak, with mechanical linking words.

Introduction: University is very important for students. Some people think students can study anything they want at university.❌ Weak paraphrase — almost identical to the prompt; no original vocabulary. Other people think students should only study useful subjects like science and technology. I will discuss both views.❌ No clear thesis — the writer's own opinion is missing from the introduction, which is required for a Band 7+ discussion essay.

Body 1: On the one hand, students should study what they like.⚠️ Topic sentence is too simplistic — no development of the underlying idea. This is because if they like the subject, they will study hard. For example, my friend likes art and he studies art at university. He is very happy.❌ Personal anecdote is too informal and not persuasive in academic writing. Also, if students study what they like, they can get good jobs. Many people study art and get good jobs.

The response addresses all parts of the task, though the position is somewhat mechanical. Main ideas are present but not fully developed. Introduction: The question of what students should study at university is widely debated.✓ Adequate general statement to open the essay. Some people believe that students should have the freedom to choose any subject, while others argue that they should focus on useful subjects like science and technology. In this essay, I will discuss both views and give my opinion.⚠️ Mechanical thesis — signals intent but does not preview the writer's actual position.

If everyone studied science, society would lose many important kinds of knowledge.✓ Uses a conditional structure effectively.

Science and technology are growing very fast, and there are many jobs in these areas.⚠️ Point is reasonable but generic — no specific evidence or example. In addition, these jobs usually pay well, so students can have a good life.⚠️ Vocabulary is informal — 'have a good life' could be 'enjoy financial security'.

🎯 The single most important lesson

The jump from Band 6 to Band 7 is the most common goal for IELTS candidates, and it is largely a matter of depth: developing each point with specific reasoning and evidence, stating a clear position from the introduction and never contradicting it, and using vocabulary that is precise rather than approximately right. The jump from Band 7 to Band 8 is a matter of insight: bringing an original perspective, connecting the argument to broader principles, and writing with the kind of effortless sophistication that comes from wide reading. Most candidates need to focus on the first leap; the second is the natural reward of genuine intellectual engagement with the topics you write about.

✍️ Model Paragraphs

Paragraph on causes of climate change
The primary driver of climate change is the combustion of fossil fuels — coal, oil, and natural gas — which releases carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere at a rate far exceeding the Earth's natural capacity to absorb them. Deforestation compounds this problem by removing the trees that would otherwise act as carbon sinks, while industrial agriculture contributes significant methane and nitrous oxide emissions. The cumulative effect of these activities since the Industrial Revolution has been a rise in global average temperatures of more than one degree Celsius, with consequences that include more frequent extreme weather events, rising sea levels, and the disruption of ecosystems on which both human and non-human life depends.
Paragraph on solutions
Addressing climate change requires a coordinated combination of regulatory, economic, and technological measures. On the regulatory side, governments must set binding emissions targets and enforce them through carbon taxes or cap-and-trade systems that internalise the environmental cost of pollution. Technologically, sustained investment in renewable energy — particularly wind, solar, and storage — is essential to phase out fossil fuels without compromising economic growth. Equally important, however, is a shift in consumer behaviour, as the transition to electric vehicles, plant-based diets, and energy-efficient housing depends ultimately on millions of individual choices that no government can fully mandate.

⚖️ Common Arguments For and Against

✓ Arguments For (typical interventions)

  • A stable climate is a prerequisite for all economic activity, so environmental protection and prosperity are not truly opposed.
  • The cost of renewable energy has fallen dramatically, making the transition cheaper than continuing with fossil fuels.
  • Future generations have a moral claim to a habitable planet that current generations are obliged to respect.
  • Environmental degradation tends to harm the poorest most, so protecting the environment is also a matter of justice.

✗ Arguments Against (typical counterpoints)

  • Rapid decarbonisation may cause short-term economic disruption, particularly in fossil-fuel-dependent regions.
  • Developing countries argue that wealthy nations industrialised without constraints and should not impose limits on others.
  • Some environmental regulations disproportionately burden small businesses and rural communities.
  • Technological solutions (e.g., carbon capture) may prove more cost-effective than behavioural change.

🎯 Likely Prompt Variations

  • Some people believe that individuals can do little to improve the environment and that only governments and large companies can make a difference. To what extent do you agree or disagree?
  • In many countries, the amount of plastic waste is increasing. What are the causes of this problem, and what solutions can you suggest?
  • Some people think that economic growth should be prioritised even if it harms the environment, while others believe that environmental protection should come first. Discuss both views and give your opinion.
  • Many species of animals and plants are in danger of extinction. What are the causes of this problem, and what solutions can you suggest?
  • In recent years, the consumption of plastic has increased significantly. Why is this happening? What can be done to reduce plastic consumption?

✍️ Model Paragraphs

Paragraph on benefits of technology
The benefits of modern technology, when properly harnessed, extend across nearly every dimension of human life. In medicine, advances in imaging, genomics, and artificial intelligence have enabled earlier diagnosis of disease and the development of treatments that would have been inconceivable a generation ago. In education, digital platforms have democratised access to knowledge, allowing a student in a remote village to attend lectures from the world's leading universities. In commerce, e-commerce and digital payments have lowered the cost of doing business and integrated previously isolated communities into the global economy. These gains, however, are not automatic; they depend on deliberate policy choices that ensure the benefits are widely shared rather than concentrated among a small technological elite.
Paragraph on risks of technology
The risks posed by rapid technological change are equally substantial. Paragraph on the purpose of education

Paragraph on the proper role of government
The question of how far government should intervene in the lives of its citizens is one of the oldest in political philosophy, and reasonable people continue to reach different conclusions. On one side stand those who argue that the state's proper role is narrow — to protect individuals from violence, enforce contracts, and provide the few public goods that markets cannot supply — leaving the greatest possible scope for individual liberty and voluntary exchange. On the other side are those who contend that genuine freedom requires more than the absence of coercion; it requires the conditions in which people can develop their capabilities, conditions that include decent education, healthcare, and a basic standard of living that only collective provision can guarantee. The most successful modern societies have tended to occupy a middle ground, combining dynamic market economies with robust social safety nets, suggesting that the choice between liberty and equality is a false one.
Paragraph on crime and punishment
Approaches to crime and punishment reveal a society's deeper assumptions about human nature and justice, and the contrast between different national philosophies is instructive. Systems that prioritise rehabilitation, by contrast — such as those in Norway and other Nordic countries — invest heavily in education, mental health treatment, and reintegration support, achieving reoffending rates roughly a third of those in more punitive systems. The choice between these approaches is ultimately as much moral as practical: whether society's primary obligation is to punish wrongdoing or to prevent its recurrence, and whether the dignity of even those who have broken the law deserves the same protection as that of their victims.

Paragraph on overtourism
Overtourism — the situation in which popular destinations receive more visitors than they can sustainably absorb — has become one of the most visible problems of the modern travel industry. Paragraph on rehabilitation vs punishment
The contrast between punishment and rehabilitation as approaches to crime reflects fundamentally different assumptions about human nature and the purpose of the criminal justice system. Punitive systems, exemplified by the United States, prioritise long sentences and strict enforcement, achieving certain reductions in crime through deterrence and incapacitation but at the cost of producing some of the highest recidivism rates in the developed world. Rehabilitation-focused systems, exemplified by Norway and other Nordic countries, invest heavily in education, mental health treatment, and reintegration support, achieving reoffending rates roughly a third of those in more punitive systems. The choice between these approaches is ultimately as much moral as practical: whether society's primary obligation is to punish wrongdoing or to prevent its recurrence, and whether the dignity of even those who have broken the law deserves the same protection as that of their victims.
Paragraph on the causes of crime
Crime is rarely the result of individual moral failure alone; it is most often the product of social and economic conditions that shape the choices available to individuals. Poverty, unemployment, and inadequate education narrow the legitimate opportunities available to young people, particularly in marginalised communities, while the breakdown of family and community structures removes the social bonds that discourage offending. Mental illness, addiction, and trauma are also significant contributors, with prisons often functioning as de facto mental health facilities. Effective crime reduction therefore requires not only a functioning justice system but also sustained investment in the social determinants of crime — education, employment, housing, and mental health support. The countries that have most successfully reduced crime have done so not through harsher sentencing but through tackling its root causes.

Brainstorming Framework

Most Band 6 candidates do not fail on language — they fail on having nothing to say. This page gives you a five-minute idea-generation workflow that works for any Task 2 prompt, with worked examples for each of the five essay types. Master this, and you will never stare at a blank page again.

🧠 The 5-Step Brainstorming Method

Use this method in the first 5 minutes after reading the prompt. Do not skip steps. Most candidates who run out of ideas do so because they skipped step 3 (the "why" layer) and went straight to writing.

1

Decode the question (30 seconds)

Identify the essay type (Opinion, Discussion, Problem/Solution, Adv/Disadv, Direct Question) and underline every part you must answer. Most low-band responses lose marks not because of weak English but because they failed to answer all parts of the question.

2

Generate 6 raw ideas (1 minute)

Write down six possible points about the topic — three on each side if it is a discussion or opinion essay, or three causes + three solutions for problem/solution. Do not evaluate them yet; quantity is more important than quality at this stage. Use the topic deep-dive pages to anticipate common arguments.

3

Push each idea one layer deeper (2 minutes)

For each raw idea, ask "Why?" or "How?" once. This is the single most important step. Most Band 6 paragraphs say what they think; Band 7+ paragraphs explain why they think it. For example, "remote work saves time" becomes "remote work saves commuting time, which lets parents spend more of the day with their children, improving both their wellbeing and the children's development."

4

Match each idea to a real example (1 minute)

For each idea, attach a concrete example — a country, a study, a personal observation, a hypothetical scenario. Examiners reward specificity. "For example" is the most useful phrase in Task 2 writing. If you cannot find an example for an idea, drop the idea.

5

Select your two strongest points (30 seconds)

You only need two body paragraphs. Pick the two ideas with the clearest reasoning and the most concrete examples. Drop the rest. Trying to fit three underdeveloped points into two paragraphs is a common Band 6 mistake.

💭 Worked Example: Opinion Essay

Prompt: "Some people believe that unpaid community service should be a compulsory part of high school programmes. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"

Step 1 — Decode: Opinion essay. Must take a clear position (agree/disagree/partial). Need 2 reasons + examples.

Step 2 — Raw ideas (6):
• Agree: builds character
• Agree: teaches real-world skills
• Agree: helps the community
• Disagree: takes time from academic subjects
• Disagree: students may resent forced volunteering
• Disagree: hard to organise safely

Step 3 — Push deeper (Why/How?):
• Builds character → because students see social realities beyond their own lives, which develops empathy and civic responsibility
• Teaches skills → teamwork, communication, problem-solving in real contexts that classrooms cannot replicate
• Takes time from academics → but only 2-3 hours/month is a small cost compared with the lifelong benefits

Step 4 — Examples:
• Character → students volunteering at homeless shelters develop understanding of poverty that textbooks cannot convey
• Skills → many employers cite teamwork and communication as the most-valued graduate skills
• Time cost → Singapore's mandatory CCA programme shows it can fit alongside rigorous academics

Step 5 — Select two: (1) Builds character through exposure to social realities; (2) Develops transferable skills valued by employers. Drop the time-cost counterargument (concede it briefly instead).

⚖️ Worked Example: Discussion Essay

Prompt: "Some people think that the best way to reduce crime is to give longer prison sentences. Others believe there are better alternative ways. Discuss both views and give your opinion."

Step 1 — Decode: Discussion essay. Must give BOTH views fair treatment and state my own opinion. Two body paragraphs: one per view.

Step 2 — Raw ideas:
View 1 (longer sentences): deters potential offenders / incapacitates convicted ones / justice for victims
View 2 (alternatives): rehabilitation reduces reoffending / addresses root causes / cheaper than prison

Step 3 — Push deeper:
• Deterrent → potential offenders weigh the cost; if sentences are longer, the cost is higher
• Rehabilitation → if prison addresses addiction, mental health, and skills, ex-offenders can reintegrate rather than reoffend

Step 4 — Examples:
• Deterrent → US "three strikes" laws correlate with reduced certain crimes (though disputed)
• Rehabilitation → Norway's recidivism rate (~20%) vs US (~67%) is striking evidence

Step 5 — Select: Body 1 = deterrent/incapacitation argument (with US example). Body 2 = rehabilitation argument (with Norway example). My opinion: rehabilitation is more effective for most offences, though long sentences remain necessary for violent crime.

🧩 Worked Example: Problem/Solution Essay

Prompt: "In many cities, traffic congestion is a growing problem. What are the causes of this problem, and what measures could be taken to solve it?"

Step 1 — Decode: Problem/Solution. Body 1 = causes; Body 2 = solutions. Each must connect logically.

Step 2 — Raw causes: too many private cars / poor public transport / urban sprawl / online delivery vans
Step 2 — Raw solutions: invest in transit / congestion charges / remote work / cargo bikes

Step 3 — Push deeper:
• Private cars → because in cities with limited transit, commuters have no choice but to drive
• Congestion charges → because they make drivers pay for the social cost they impose, shifting them to alternatives

Step 4 — Examples:
• Cars cause → Bangkok's limited suburban transit forces high car dependence
• Congestion charges work → London's congestion charge reduced central traffic by ~30%

Step 5 — Select: Cause = reliance on private cars due to inadequate transit (Bangkok). Solution = combined investment in transit + congestion pricing (Seoul, London). The solution logically addresses the cause.

⚡ Worked Example: Advantages/Disadvantages Essay

Prompt: "Many people now work from home rather than in an office. Do the advantages of this trend outweigh the disadvantages?"

Step 1 — Decode: Adv/Disadv (outweigh version). Must give BOTH sides AND state which outweighs. Cannot be neutral.

Step 2 — Raw ideas:
Advantages: saves commute time / better work-life balance / lower office costs / environmental benefits
Disadvantages: weaker collaboration / isolation / home distractions / blurred work-home boundary

Step 3 — Push deeper:
• Saves commute time → 9 hours/week average (UK survey) redirected to family, exercise, study → measurable wellbeing gains
• Weaker collaboration → but mitigated by hybrid models (1-2 office days/week) and digital tools

Step 4 — Examples:
• Time saved → 2022 UK recruitment survey: 9 hrs/week saved on average
• Hybrid mitigation → many firms report productivity maintained with 1-2 office days

Step 5 — Select: Body 1 = advantage (time saved, wellbeing, environment). Body 2 = disadvantage (collaboration loss) + weighing comment (mitigated by hybrid). Position: advantages outweigh, with mindful implementation.

❓ Worked Example: Direct Question (Two-Part) Essay

Prompt: "In many countries, fewer young people are choosing to read for pleasure. Why do you think this is happening? What can be done to encourage reading among young people?"

Step 1 — Decode: Direct Question. Two questions: WHY (cause) + WHAT (solution). Body 1 = cause; Body 2 = solution. NOT a problem/solution essay — the two questions need not be a cause-and-effect pair.

Step 2 — Raw causes: smartphones compete for attention / school pressure leaves no time / books seen as old-fashioned / parents don't model reading
Step 2 — Raw solutions: student-chosen reading in school / family reading time / library funding / booktubers & influencer promotion

Step 3 — Push deeper:
• Smartphones compete → short-form content demands less sustained attention than a novel; habit of fragmentation
• Student-chosen reading → autonomy + interest drives engagement, unlike prescribed classics

Step 4 — Examples:
• Smartphone competition → teenagers in 1990s read on commute; today they scroll short videos
• Student-chosen reading → Finland's programme improved both frequency and comprehension

Step 5 — Select: Q1 answer = smartphone competition (attention fragmentation). Q2 answer = student-chosen reading in schools (Finland example). Both questions answered distinctly.

💡 Pro Tips

  • Always write the brainstorm down — do not try to hold it in your head. The 5 minutes you "save" by skipping this step will cost you 10 minutes of mid-essay stuckness.
  • The "Why?" layer is what separates Band 6 from Band 7. If your body paragraph has only a "what" but no "why", push it deeper.
  • Examples do not need to be true — they need to be plausible and specific. "A 2022 survey by a UK firm found…" is fine. "Some people think" is not.
  • Drop ideas you cannot support. Two strong points always beat three weak ones.
  • For discussion essays, force yourself to argue each side fairly. If you cannot articulate the opposing view convincingly, you do not understand the topic well enough.

Paraphrasing Techniques

Paraphrasing is the single most underestimated skill in IELTS Writing Task 2. It directly affects two of the four marking criteria: Task Achievement (you must paraphrase the prompt in your introduction) and Lexical Resource (you must avoid repeating the same words throughout). Master these six techniques and your score will rise.

🎯 Where paraphrasing matters most

1. The introduction — your first sentence must restate the prompt in your own words; copying it directly is an instant Band 6 ceiling.
2. Topic sentences — body paragraph openers should not repeat the prompt verbatim.
3. Recurring key terms — if the prompt is about "remote work", do not write "remote work" ten times; vary it ("working from home", "telecommuting", "off-site working", "distributed work").

Technique 1

Synonym Substitution

The simplest technique: replace key words with their synonyms. Most useful for the introduction's opening sentence, but use sparingly — relying only on synonyms produces stilted writing.

Before Many people believe that social media has a negative effect on society.
After A significant proportion of the population holds the view that social networking platforms have an adverse impact on communities.
Before The government should invest more money in public transport.
After The state ought to allocate greater funding to public transit systems.
Technique 2

Active ↔ Passive Voice

Switching between active and passive voice changes sentence structure without changing meaning. Particularly useful in academic writing, where the passive voice can sound more objective.

Before Governments should ban single-use plastics to reduce ocean pollution.
After Single-use plastics should be prohibited by governments in order to reduce marine pollution.
Before Many companies have adopted flexible working hours.
After Flexible working hours have been adopted by a large number of companies.
Technique 3

Clause Restructuring

Change the grammatical structure: turn a clause into a phrase, a noun into a verb, or reorder the parts of the sentence. This is the most powerful technique because it changes the rhythm of your writing.

Before Because technology develops rapidly, laws cannot keep up.
After The rapid pace of technological development outstrips the ability of lawmakers to respond.
Before Students who study abroad gain valuable experience.
After The experience of studying abroad is invaluable for students.
Technique 4

Change of Perspective

Rewrite the sentence from a different viewpoint — start with the result rather than the cause, or with the affected group rather than the actor. This often produces the most natural-sounding paraphrases.

Before Rising house prices make it difficult for young people to buy their first home.
After Young people increasingly struggle to step onto the property ladder as a result of escalating housing costs.
Before Air pollution causes respiratory disease in urban populations.
After Urban populations suffer disproportionately from respiratory disease caused by poor air quality.
Technique 5

Summarising / Generalising

Replace specific phrases with more general categories. Useful when you need to compress a long prompt into a single introductory sentence.

Before Some people think that mathematics, physics, and chemistry should receive more government funding than history, literature, and philosophy.
After Some argue that scientific disciplines merit greater public investment than the humanities.
Before People are using cars, buses, trains, and planes more than ever before.
After The use of motorised transport has reached unprecedented levels.
Technique 6

Definition Restatement

Restate a term by describing what it means, rather than naming it. This technique forces precision and is especially useful for abstract concepts.

Before Globalisation has transformed the world economy.
After The growing integration of national economies through trade, investment, and technology has reshaped the global economic landscape.
Before Artificial intelligence will disrupt many industries.
After Computer systems capable of performing tasks once thought to require human intelligence are likely to transform a wide range of sectors.

⚠️ Common Paraphrasing Mistakes

  • Changing meaning while changing words — a paraphrase must preserve the original meaning exactly. "Difficult" and "impossible" are not synonyms.
  • Overusing unusual synonyms — using "pulchritudinous" for "beautiful" makes your writing sound unnatural. Stick to words you would actually use in speech.
  • Forgetting to vary repeated key terms — if the prompt is about "university education", use "higher education", "tertiary study", "university-level learning" to avoid repetition.
  • Paraphrasing only the introduction — the same skill applies throughout the essay. Watch for words you have used more than twice.
  • Copying long phrases from the prompt — three or more consecutive words from the prompt count as copied and will not contribute to your Lexical Resource score.
